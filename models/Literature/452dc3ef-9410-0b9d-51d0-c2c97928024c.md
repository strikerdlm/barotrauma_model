# Predictors Of Ear Barotrauma In Aircrews Exposed To Simulated High Altitude

 Fabio Morgagni , Alberto Autore , Angelo Landolfi , Mario Ciniglio Appiani , and Giuseppe Ciniglio Appiani M ORGAGNI F, A UTORE A, L ANDOLFI A, C INIGLIO A PPIANI M, 
C INIGLIO A PPIANI G. *Predictors of ear barotrauma in aircrews exposed* to simulated high altitude. **Aviat Space Environ Med 2012; 83: 594 - 7 .** 
Introduction: Ear barotrauma is an adverse effect related to hypobaric exposure. Ear, nose, and throat (ENT) diseases are risk factors for barotrauma in aircrews trained in a hypobaric chamber, but excluding affected subjects from exposure does not abolish the risk in asymptomatic trainees. We investigated other possible predictors, including history of ENT diseases, ENT clinical abnormalities, altitude, and subject ' s age. 

Methods: After a complete ENT evaluation including otoscopy and tympanometry, 314 aircrews underwent hypobaric chamber training. Two altitude training profi les up to 35,000 ft (10,668 m) and 25,000 ft (7620 m), respectively, were used. Subjects were grouped according to if they were asymptomatic, had acute barotitis, or reported delayed ear pain the day after the exposure. *Results:* There were 7 men who had acute barotitis (incidence of 2.3%) and 28 men who had delayed ear pain (incidence of 9.2%). A signifi cant association resulted between history of ENT diseases and delayed ear pain and between abnormal ENT fi ndings and acute barotitis in subjects exposed to the higher profi le. Altitude was associated with increased risk of delayed ear pain. Delayed ear pain was associated with older age in subjects exposed to the lower altitude and younger age in subjects exposed to the higher altitude. *Discussion:* Our data suggest that in subjects exposed to 35,000 ft (10,668 m), the history of previous ENT diseases and younger age may be valid predictors of delayed ear pain, while abnormal ENT fi ndings may predict acute barotitis. At 25,000 ft (7620 m), subjects with older age may have increased risk of delayed ear pain. Keywords: ear barotrauma , altitude chamber .

A tympanogram may be useful in detecting poorly functioning/no patent ETs ( 4 ), but it appears to be unserviceable as predictor ( 1 ). The previous history of ear, nose, and throat (ENT) diseases has also been investigated through questionnaires ( 2,10 ), but its helpfulness is controversial. We introduced medical screening of trainees just before exposure in a hypobaric chamber. This improvement, which includes otoscopy and tympanometry, resulted in an effective reduction in the incidence of acute barotitis via identifying in advance the subjects at risk ( 8 ), who are not regularly cleared for high altitude exposure in our institution for this reason. Despite this measure, the risk of ear barotrauma was not abolished in our population and, because of this, we investigated a set of possible predictors. Our hypothesis was that previous history of ENT diseases, abnormal ENT fi ndings before the exposure, altitude, and the subject ' s age could be predictors of acute ear barotrauma or delayed ear pain in healthy aircrews trained in high altitude in a hypobaric chamber.

The incidence of acute barotitis in aircrews trained in our institution is 2.4% ( 7 ), but other authors reported different values ( 9,3 ). The reasons for such variable incidence among studies include differences in altitude change rates and diagnostic criteria for ear barotrauma, and if subjects received or did not receive a medical evaluation before and after the exposure. We have also described the delayed ear pain that is frequently reported by subjects exposed to high altitude while breathing oxygen ( 6 ). Impaired function of the Eustachian tube (ET) in otherwise healthy airmen is thought to be a major determining factor of a mismatch between changes in barometric pressure and the capability of the ET in balancing the pressure in the middle ear. A moderate altitude change rate and active maneuvers such as Valsalva or swallowing are effective countermeasures against the risk of barotrauma.

## Procedure

 The altitude chamber was a model made by AMST 
Systemtechnik GmbH (Ranshofen, Austria). All subjects received a complete ENT examination by a physician before the altitude exposure. A tympanogram was recorded in each subject from both ears before entering the altitude chamber and ET function was assessed with the Valsalva maneuver or Toynbee ' s test. Out of 314 subjects, 211 were trained with the Type 1 profi le consisting of an ascent up to 25,000 ft (7620 m) at 4000 ft (1219.2 m) per min followed by demonstration of acute hypoxia, then descent to 18,000 ft (5846.4 m) for demonstration of visual impairment due to hypoxia, and then a return to ground at 2500 ft (762 m) per min ( **Fig. 1** ). The remaining 

Copyright: Aerospace Medical Association 103 subjects were trained with the Type 2 profi le, which differed from the previous because the maximum altitude was set at 35,000 ft (10,668 m) for a positive pressure breathing demonstration. All trainees were asked to periodically swallow during the ascent or perform a Valsalva maneuver on descent, as is usual in the altitude training protocol. Subjects were also invited to perform the Valsalva maneuver every hour after the exposure until going to sleep in order to facilitate the washout of oxygen from the middle ear. 

 History was considered positive if a subject revealed previous episodes of ear barotrauma, acute or chronic otitis, allergic rhinitis, or recent or recurrent upper respiratory tract infections. ENT fi ndings were considered abnormal if any of the following were detected by the doctor: abnormal otoscopy, redness and congestion of the nasal mucosa, or redness of the pharynx with direct observation. Clearly, these abnormalities were so mild that subjects were cleared for high altitude exposure. 

 Subjects were grouped as asymptomatic, those with acute barotitis, and those who reported ear pain the day after the exposure. Criteria for acute barotitis included ear pain during or immediately after the exposure, along with one of the following: abnormal otoscopy according to the Teed ' s classifi cation ( 11 ), or a C or B type tympanogram ( 5 ). Delayed ear pain was defi ned as ear pain with onset more than 2 h after the end of exposure or at awakening the morning after, possibly with abnormal otoscopy or tympanogram. Differences between groups were analyzed with ANOVA or Fisher exact test as appropriate. The level of signifi cance was set at P , 0.05.

These fi ndings support the evidence that the previous history of ENT disease may be a valid predictor of delayed ear pain and ENT abnormalities predict acute barotitis in subjects exposed to 35,000 ft (10,668 m). 

 According to our data, younger age was predictive of delayed ear pain in subjects trained at the higher altitude, while older age was predictive of delayed ear pain in subjects exposed to the lower altitude. One would expect that older individuals might have impaired ET function due to stiffness of the tube and this could explain the higher prevalence of delayed ear pain in older subjects at the lower altitude. Unfortunately, this is in contrast with the outcome at the higher altitude in our study. Moreover, previous studies did not demonstrate any age-related difference in ET function in subjects below 40 yr and the average age difference among our groups was within 5 yr, which is not likely to explain signifi cant changes in the stiffness of the ET. An alternative explanation is that our younger and inexperienced subjects may not have complied with the invitation to periodically perform the Valsalva maneuver after the exposure, or did it inappropriately. Clearly, because our subjects left our laboratory 3 hr after the training session, we could not control whether or how subjects performed the Valsalva maneuver. 

 Although not considered a tool for prediction according to the literature, tympanometry before exposure was not helpful in this study. However, it was useful in identifying eight subjects at risk who were not cleared for the exposure. An explanation is that the mild impairment of ET function recorded in subjects who were then admitted to the altitude chamber did not necessarily prevent a suffi cient ventilation of the middle ear or reduce the effectiveness of the Valsalva maneuver. Obviously, we cannot exclude that the eight subjects not cleared for the exposure might not have had barotrauma after the training session. 

 We studied a group of consecutive subjects exposed to simulated high altitude as part of the aerophysiological training for aircrews which is mandatory in the Italian Air Force.  The major limit of our study is that younger subjects were trained with the higher profi le. This resulted in the exposure of inexperienced individuals to more challenging environmental conditions. A second limit is that women were under-represented in our population and no woman had acute barotitis or delayed ear pain. There are still few women among our aviators and this limitation affects all our investigations. Thus, as expected, our study provided no information about women. Finally, this study included procedures that are a regular part of the training protocol. This could be a limit because no advanced investigations were used to assess the function of the Eustachian tube, such as the Bluestone nine-step test. The advantage is that our protocol, which is based on personal history, graded otoscopy according to Teed ' s classifi cation and a tympanogram can also be easily executed by a nurse. 

 In conclusion, our data suggest that the previous history of ENT disease may be a predictor of delayed ear pain while abnormal ENT fi ndings may predict acute barotitis in healthy men exposed to a simulated altitude of 35,000 ft (10,668 m) in a hypobaric chamber. Older age may be also helpful as a general predictor of delayed ear pain, although younger subjects appear to have increased risk of delayed ear pain when exposed to very high altitude.
