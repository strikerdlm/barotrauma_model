# Altitude Chamber Related Adverse Effects Among 1241 Airmen

 Fabio Morgagni , Alberto Autore , Angelo Landolfi , Francesco Torchia , and Giuseppe Ciniglio Appiani M ORGAGNI F, A UTORE A, L ANDOLFI A, T ORCHIA F, C INIGLIO A PPIANI
G. *Altitude chamber related adverse effects among 1241 airmen.* **Aviat** Space Environ Med 2010; 81: 873 - 7 . 

Introduction: Altitude chambers are used for training aircrews, but incidents have been reported, including decompression sickness (DCS) 
and barotrauma. To minimize chamber-related adverse effects we implemented a set of measures, including altitude restriction and a prechamber clinical selection (PCS) of subjects before exposure. *Methods:*
We reviewed our records regarding 1254 individuals who were trained from 2003 to 2009. After the fi rst 3 subjects, the maximum altitude of the highest training profi le was limited to 43,000 ft(13,106.4 m) instead of 45,000 ft (13,716 m) and, after the fi rst 327 subjects, a clinical evaluation of each trainee was performed by an otolaryngologist before altitude exposure. The evaluation included otoscopy and tympanometry, and subjects with abnormal results were not cleared for altitude exposure. Subjects were grouped by having undergone the highest profi le before (3 subjects) or after altitude restriction (8 subjects) and received clinical selection (PCS group, 927 subjects) or not (control group, 327 subjects). Results: We recorded 32 total adverse effects (overall incidence 2.6%), 21 in the PCS group (2.3%) and 11 in the control group (3.4%). The difference between groups was not signifi cant. Adverse effects included 19 cases of acute barotitis (1.5%), 1 case of DCS (0.08%), and 4 cases of syncope (0.3%). The incidence of barotitis was 1.1% in the PCS 
group and 2.7% in the control group. The altitude restriction was ineffective in preventing both barotrauma and DCS. *Conclusions:* The incidence of adverse effects in our subjects was low and prechamber clinical selection appeared to be effective in reducing the risk of barotitis. Keywords: altitude , hypobaric chamber , high altitude training . 

 ALTITUDE CHAMBERS are widely used for training in a hypoxic and hypobaric environment, which is a mandatory part of aerophysiological training for military fl ight crews in Italy. The purpose of altitude training is to acquire experience of the environmental challenges that can affect fl ight safety, such as hypoxia and hypobarism. There is evidence that people who have experienced hypoxia respond better to in-fl ight decompression ( 7 ). Because symptoms of hypoxia tend to be constant in the same individual ( 6 ), there is no alternative to its direct demonstration. However, since low-pressure chambers became available in aeromedical practice, many operators have reported several incidents, generating criticism over the safety of this equipment ( 5 ). 

 The major concern regards decompression sickness 
(DCS) ( 14,8 ), the mean incidence of which is between 0.01% and 0.25% ( 10,2,11 ), with a peak of 0.39% ( 13 ). Preventive denitrogenation with 100% oxygen breathing has a protective effect ( 11 ). It has also been reported that the incidence of altitude chamber-related DCS is noticeably increasing and is somewhat higher among inside observers than in trainees ( 10,2 ), although these data are controversial ( 16 ). The risk of DCS appears to be greater under experimental conditions than during operational fl ights ( 16 ). 

 A second group of adverse effects is represented by barotrauma ( 9,4 ), mainly involving the middle ear. The reported incidence of ear barotrauma after altitude chamber exposure varies ( 3 ), but ear pain alone is often considered as a near normal reaction rather than an adverse effect and in most cases no further clinical assessment is carried out. Moreover, subjects undergoing altitude chamber training may fail to report mild disturbances in order to avoid repercussions for their certifi cation. For these reasons there may be a lack of data regarding ear barotrauma and its true incidence is generally underestimated. 

 Alternative training tools, such as reduced oxygen mixtures breathing (ROB) devices ( 12,1 ), are useful for avoiding both DCS and barotrauma. ROB devices can also be combined with advanced fl ight simulators to provide more realistic scenarios, but because of a number of major limitations this equipment is not currently an entirely satisfactory alternative to altitude chambers. 

In order to minimize the risk of altitude chamberrelated DCS and barotrauma, we implemented a set of measures, including limiting maximum altitude to 43,000 ft (13,106.4 m) and a pre-chamber selection of trainees. The null hypothesis was that these measures are ineffective.

## Methods

 We reviewed our database of altitude chamber training from April 2003 to April 2009 in order to assess retrospectively the effectiveness of our procedures. 

## Subjects

 Subjects were fl ight personnel, mainly from the Italian Air Force, but also from other branches of the Italian Armed Forces and those of other countries. All subjects signed an informed consent form before participating in altitude training. All training sessions were conducted in the Aerospace Medicine Department of the ITAF Flight Test Center. Because the study was retrospective, all procedures were part of a standard altitude training, and no further invasive procedures were added to the protocol. The study was approved by the local Institutional Review Board. 

## Equipment

 The hypobaric chamber was a model developed by AMST Systemtechnik GmbH - Ranshofen (Austria). It was operated according to the instructions released by the factory and in compliance with the safety requirements regarding pressure driven systems laid down by the European Union and Italy. A dedicated hyperbaric chamber was available on site for the treatment of DCS. 

Electrocardiogram and blood saturation were continuously recorded for each subject inside the chamber with wireless transmitter-receiver based equipment (Dyna Scope 5700 central monitor and wireless peripherals, LX-5230, Fukuda Denshi Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan ).

## Procedures

 The aims of the altitude training included: detection and recognition of the individual symptoms of hypoxia, enhancement of internal gas expansion countermeasures, and demonstration of sudden decompression. 

Practicing how to operate the oxygen supply system and the demonstration of positive pressure breathing were also part of the training. During exposures, blood saturation and electrocardiograms of all subjects were continuously monitored. A supervising physician and a nurse who had both been specially trained for hypobaric chamber operations were always present on site, together with a specialized hyperbaric team composed of a nurse and two qualifi ed technicians for profi les over 18,000 ft (5486.4 m). 

 All personnel permitted to undergo altitude training were certifi ed for fl ight duties by the ITAF Aeromedical Institute. After providing an accurate personal history, all trainees received a series of lectures on physiology from an aeromedical point of view and a briefi ng on chamber-related emergencies. Before each profi le a brief ascent up to 5000 ft (1524 m) followed by return to ground level was performed in order to assess the ability of the trainees to balance the middle ear and sinus pressure changes (sinus check). Subjects who did not pass the sinus check were discharged. 

 Each session started with 30 min of denitrogenation with 100% oxygen breathing before ascent in order to minimize the risk of DCS. The climb rate was set at 4000 ft z min 21 (1219.2 m z min 21 ) and the descent rate at 2500 ft z min 21 (762 m z min 21 ) for all profi les in order to reduce the risk of barotrauma while maintaining the need to balance the pressure changes. Criteria for the conclusion of the hypoxia demonstration were: two or more symptoms of hypoxia reported by the trainees, blood saturation of 55%, or time spent in hypoxia of 4 min at 25,000 ft (7620 m) and 15 min at 18,000 ft (5486.4 m). 

 Four training protocols were used: Type 1, Type 2, Type 2a, and Type 3. After the sinus check, the Type 1 profi le proceeded with ascent up to 25,000 ft (7620 m). At this altitude, the trainees removed their masks and performed a set of tasks until two or more symptoms of hypoxia were detected. Then they donned the mask and restored the oxygen supply. After descent to 18,000 ft 
(5486.4 m), the lights were attenuated and the trainees removed their masks again for demonstration of night vision impairment. Finally, the profi le concluded with return to ground level. 

 The Type 2 and Type 2a profi les were different from the previous protocol; altitudes were 35,000 ft (10,668 m) and 43,000 ft (13,106.4 m), respectively. After the fi rst three subjects trained with the Type 2a profi le, the maximum altitude was set at 43,000 ft (13,106.4 m) instead of 45,000 ft (13,716 m) in order to reduce the risk of decompression sickness. The Type 3 profi le consisted of a sudden decompression simulation with a climb from 8000 ft 
(2438.4 m) to 22,000 ft (6705.6 m) in less than 3 s while the trainees experienced sudden gas expansion, noise, and fog. It followed one of the previous profi les within 5 to 20 min. 

 Subjects who reported symptoms of acute barotrauma or any other problem during the previous exposure were not usually cleared for rapid decompression. All trainees were required to remain in the facility for at least 1 h after chamber exposure and those suffering any discomfort were subjected to postfl ight clinical evaluation by a physician immediately and again the following day. After 327 subjects we implemented a pre-chamber clinical selection (PCS) with the purpose of excluding subjects at risk of barotrauma. Each trainee underwent examination by an otolaryngologist, including otoscopy and a tympanogram, just before chamber exposure. The tympanometry focused mainly on assessment of the functional capacity of the Eustachian tube. Subjects showing abnormal clinical or tympanometric results were not cleared for chamber exposure. 

 The diagnostic criteria for decompression sickness included the presence of joint pain, marbled skin, or chokes (Type I DCS), or neurological impairment (Type II DCS) during or after chamber exposure. Barotrauma was diagnosed if acute ear, facial, or abdominal pain occurred during ascent or descent without relief within a few minutes or after chamber arrest. ## Statistics

 Subjects were grouped for analysis as follows: subjects who were trained with the Type 2a profi le before 
(three subjects) or after (eight subjects) the adoption of altitude limitation, and those who underwent prechamber clinical selection (PCS group, 927 subjects), or not (control group, 327 subjects) before altitude exposure. Data were analyzed with the Chi-square test and the odds ratio was computed. 

## Results

 A total of 171 hypobaric chamber sessions were performed, with 1598 subjects scheduled for exposures, including 1254 trainees and 344 inside observers. Because no incidents were reported by the inside observers, they were excluded from the analysis. There were originally 927 subjects in the PCS group. Six subjects from the PCS group (intragroup prevalence 0.6%) were not cleared for chamber exposure after the pre-chamber selection. Seven subjects were discharged after the sinus check, three from the PCS group (0.3%) and four from the control group (1.2%), and this difference was not statistically signifi cant (odds ratio 0.27, 95% CI 0.06 to 1.20, P 5 0.06). A total of 1241 subjects ages 30.0 6 9.6 yr, which included 1223 men and 18 women, fi nally underwent simulated altitude exposure ( **Table I** ). The mean age of female personnel was 22.5 6 0.7. The fi nal group sizes were 918 for the PCS group (906 men and 12 women, mean age 29.3 6 9.7) and 323 in the control group (317 men and 6 women, mean age 30.2 6 9.5). 

 We recorded 32 adverse effects with an overall incidence of 2.6% ( **Table II** ). The adverse effects in the PCS 
group accounted for 21 cases (2.3%), including 1 woman, and 11 cases were in the control group (3.4%, no women). The difference between groups was not signifi cant (odds ratio 0.66, 95% CI 0.32 to 1.39, P 5 0.28). The only case of an adverse effect among female personnel was an acute barotitis which occurred in a student pilot from the PCS group.

 We found an interestingly high occurrence of syncope, with four cases and an overall incidence of 0.3% of the total population. It presented as acute symptomatic bradycardia or loss of consciousness and was always observed immediately after the supply of oxygen was restored following the demonstration of hypoxia. All four cases occurred in the PCS group. 

 Among the other eight incidents, two cases of abdominal pain and two of ear pain recovered immediately when the chamber was leveled out, and the training was then successfully completed without further problems. ## Discussion

 The overall incidence of altitude chamber-related adverse effects was 2.6% in our subjects compared with the mean prevalence reported by other authors between 1.3% and 6.3% ( 3,4,9 ). Altitude limitation had no statistically signifi cant effect on all adverse reactions in general. Seven subjects in the PCS group were not cleared for altitude exposure because they were recognized as potentially at risk of acute barotitis. Our data did not reach statistical signifi cance even adding the subjects discharged at PCS to those who had barotitis. The number of subjects discharged after sinus check was lower in the PCS group without statistical signifi cance. 

 Barotrauma occurred mainly as acute barotitis and its incidence was 1.5%, less than the reported prevalence of ear pain/ear block between 2.1% ( 4 ) and 4.7% ( 9 ). The incidence of acute barotitis in the PCS group was lower than in the control group, although statistical signifi -
cance is questionable. In fact, according to Yates' correction for continuity, our results should not lead to rejection of the null hypothesis. Conversely, the odds ratio provides evidence of association between PCS and low risk of acute barotitis. Our data may be not suffi cient to achieve a clear statistical signifi cance; however, on the basis of the analysis we are encouraged to consider the pre-chamber selection effective in reducing the risk of acute barotitis in our subjects. 

 The low incidence of barotitis in our subjects could also be due to the descent rate of 2500 ft z min 21 (762 m z min 21 ). A previous study used a descent rate of 4000 ft z min 21
(1219.2 m z min 21 ), which may explain the higher occurrence of ear pain ( 9 ). A confounding factor may be that our subjects from both PCS and control group were trained with different profi les, but we have not found different outcomes related to profi les. It is possible that our trainees did not report mild ear pain in order to avoid further examinations and waiver, although this problem might also affect other studies. 

 The overall incidence of decompression sickness was 0.08% in our subjects, close to the mean reported in the literature of 0.11%. Altitude restriction had no effect in preventing DCS, but clearly only one case is not suffi - cient to draw any conclusion. The four cases of syncope in our study all occurred in the PCS group. We cannot fully explain this outcome at this moment, but it may be due to differences between the groups, as PCS is not expected to affect cardiovascular response. 

 The explanation for the onset after the oxygen supply was restored is unclear: it could be a hypoxia effect which was not immediately corrected by oxygen. We used a fi ngertip pulse oximeter, which returned the value of blood saturation with a delay that is related to physiological conditions and technical constraints. Normally, blood saturation begins to increase 5 to 10 s after the restoration of the oxygen supply because of time required for absorption and symptoms of hypoxia begin to disappear after approximately 5 s more due to the time required for oxygen delivery to cells. Moreover, changes in environmental parameters may affect the accuracy of the pulse oximeter and an error can be also related to peripheral vasoconstriction due to hypoxia. An alternative explanation is that oxygen may have had an additional vasocontrictive effect on cerebral vasculature already constricted as a result of carbon dioxide depletion following hypoxic hyperventilation. We did not record breathing parameters in standard training profi les, but one can reasonably expect an increase in ventilation related to changes in blood saturation. 

 Interestingly, we did not record any major adverse effect among inside observers. These people did not undergo the pre-chamber examination and one would expect them to suffer from at least some minor problems. It is likely that people with more experience are more capable of balancing pressure changes, but they may also fail to report mild symptoms and for this reason we chose to exclude inside observers from the analysis. 

 We adopted a pre-chamber clinical selection that consisted of ear, nose, and throat examination carried out by an otolaryngologist followed by tympanometry. This procedure could appear excessive for personnel who have just been medically certifi ed for fl ight duties and, in fact, other operators generally do not perform tympanometry. However, chronic sinusitis and mild forms of the common cold can occur in asymptomatic subjects and the possibility that trainees did not report the problem in order to complete their training without repercussions cannot be excluded. Further studies should assess the cost-effectiveness of pre-chamber selection. 

 The major limit of our study was the small population, which affects comparison with other larger studies. 

Moreover, different studies can vary in terms of subjects, equipment, and protocols, which also makes comparison somewhat diffi cult. We changed our procedures in progress in order to improve the safety and these changes did not occur at the same time. Moreover, the study mixed two procedures, altitude limitation and PCS, with different expected effects. We investigated the effect of altitude limitation among subjects trained with the Type 2a profi le only and this resulted in some of the eight subjects who were trained after the procedure had been modifi ed also being included in the PCS group. Because subjects treated with the modifi ed Type 2a profi le had no adverse effects except for one case of decompression sickness, we reasoned that the confounding effect between PCS and altitude limitation may be minor in this case. 

 In conclusion, we found that the altitude chamber is a safe and suitable environment in which to undergo
