# Recurrent Middle Ear Barotrauma In Student Pilots

Jung Heob Sohn INTRODUCTION: The present study evaluated the efficacy of the nine-step inflation-deflation tympanometric test for predicting recurrence of middle ear barotrauma.

METHODS: Student pilots who were diagnosed with middle ear barotrauma from October 2010 to April 2011 were enrolled. The grade of barotrauma was assigned using Teed's classification. All subjects underwent tympanometry and nine-step inflation-deflation testing at first visit and after improvement. Recurrence was monitored for 2 wk after they resumed flight. The relationships among the recurrence of middle ear barotrauma, the grade and duration of disease, and tympanometric and nine-step test results were evaluated.

RESULTS: There were 35 cases enrolled. According to Teed's classification, 16 cases were Grade 0 (45.7%) and 11 cases were Grade I 
(31.4%). Grade III was shown in seven cases (20.0%) and one subject was Grade IV (2.9%). The mean duration of illness was 5.9 d. In the initial 9-step tests, 29 subjects (85.3%) failed to pass the entire test. On follow-up tests, eight cases (22.9%) failed. Seven pilots (20.0%) showed recurrent middle ear barotrauma after resumed flight. All of the cases with recurrence failed the follow-up nine-step tests. The nine-step test showed high sensitivity and specificity values for predicting recurrence. A clear correlation was observed between recurrence and nine-step test results.

DISCUSSION: The nine-step inflation-deflation test provides useful information about Eustachian tube function. The nine-step test can be useful to reduce the risk of recurrence of middle ear barotrauma in pilots.

KEYWORDS: barotrauma, middle ear, recurrence, Eustachian tube.

Delivered by Ingenta Middle ear barotrauma is one of the most common medical problems in aircrews, including pilots.16 Middle ear barotrauma is mucosal inflammation of the middle ear and tympanic membrane (TM) resulting from difference between middle ear cavity air pressure and environmental air pressure, circumstances that usually develop after descent from air travel.15,18 Common symptoms include otalgia and ear fullness, accompanied by effusion in the middle ear cavity or retraction of the TM. Eustachian tube (ET) dysfunction in otherwise healthy aircrew members is considered to play a key role in the etiology of middle ear barotrauma.12 Long periods of grounding due to medical problems may have a negative impact on pilots' careers, especially those of student pilots, and may affect student pilot retention.18 When grounding is due to barotrauma, the risk of recurrence is increased if flight is resumed without complete resolution of the disease. Therefore, accurate determination of cure and the decision whether to return student pilots suffering from middle ear barotrauma to flight duty are essential. In most cases, diagnosis and resolution of middle ear barotrauma are judged only by improvement of symptoms and presence of the TM.

The nine-step inflation-deflation tympanometric test is simple and provides practical information about ET dysfunction.3 Hussein et al. reported that nine-step inflation-deflation test findings are useful predictors of middle ear barotrauma.6 The purpose of the present study was to determine whether the nine-step inflation-deflation test is also applicable for predicting recurrence of middle ear barotrauma in clinically recovered student pilots.

Daily surveillance and temporary flying restrictions were required for all subjects with middle ear barotrauma before recovery. Acetaminophen (500 mg three times daily) and pseudoephedrine (60 mg twice daily) were administered orally to relieve symptoms as the occasion warranted. Improvement was determined as resolution of symptoms and normalization of the TM (pellucid TM without any effusion or perforation). The duration from diagnosis to the time of disease improvement was also assessed.

The nine-step inflation-deflation test was performed at the subjects' first visit and again at follow-up when the pilots were assessed to be clinically improved from middle ear barotrauma. After resumed flight with improvement, an additional 2-wk monitoring period was carried out to assess recurrence. Recurrence was defined as any symptoms of middle ear barotrauma with or without positive TM findings during return to flight training. The sensitivity and specificity values of recurrence for the nine-step inflation-deflation test, which was performed before resumed flight, were evaluated. The recurrence rate was compared according to grade and duration of middle ear barotrauma and the results of initial and follow-up nine-step inflation-deflation tests.

Subjects Student pilots in the flight training wing who were diagnosed with middle ear barotrauma from October 1, 2010, to April 30, 2011, were included in this study. The diagnoses were made at the aerospace medical corps otolaryngology clinic. Prior to the flight-training program, all student pilots were confirmed to be free from any otologic diseases; their TM function and hearing ability were examined by an otolaryngologist and certified flight surgeon. Subjects who were lost to follow-up were excluded from the study, which was approved by the Institutional Ethics Committee of the institution with which the author was affiliated (approval no. 2018-09-011-002).

Procedure The eardrum of subjects complaining of persistent ear symptoms (ear fullness, ear pain, or hearing loss) was estimated using a 0°, 2.7-mm rigid endoscope (Richard Wolf Medical Instruments, Vernon Hills, IL, USA) by a single otolaryngologist. Middle ear barotrauma was assessed with objective findings of the eardrum using the Teed classification:19 Grade 0 signified no sign of barotrauma; Grade I designation indicated retraction of the TM with partial hyperemia in the Shrapnell membrane and along the manubrium; Grade II meant retraction of the TM with total hyperemia; Grade III signified hemotympanum with or without air fluid level in the middle ear; and Grade IV indicated rupture of the TM. In bilateral cases, severity was determined according to the findings on the more serious side.

All subjects underwent tympanometry via an electroacoustic impedance device, GSI TympStar version 2 (Grason-Stadler Inc., Eden Prairie, MN, USA). Tympanometry was conducted to measure the resting middle ear pressure (MEP) and then the nine-step inflation-deflation test was conducted. The impedance test is noninvasive and calculates MEP indirectly according to tympanometric peak. Tympanometric measurement of MEP has been reported to be well correlated with extant value.1 The nine-step inflation-deflation test was accomplished in the manner reported by Bluestone et al.3,14 Step 1 recorded resting MEP via the tympanogram and Step 2 measured the increase of ear canal pressure and MEP to +200 mmH2O with medial deflection of the TM. In Step 3, the subject swallowed to equilibrate middle ear over-pressure and then refrained from swallowing to return ear canal pressure to normal. This established a slightly negative MEP, documented by the tympanogram. Step 4 examined the subject's attempt to equilibrate the negative MEP by swallowing again. If equilibration was successful, airflow moved from the nasopharynx to the middle ear. 

In Step 5, the tympanogram recorded the extent of equilibrium, and in Step 6, the ear canal pressure was reduced to 2200 mmH2O, causing a corresponding decrease in MEP and lateral deflection of the TM. The subject swallowed again to equilibrate the negative MEP; if successful, airflow moved from the nasopharynx to the middle ear. In the present study, 35 cases (32 men, 3 women) were enrolled. There were 14 patients (40.0%) who complained of otalgia, and 8 (22.9%) had hearing impairment, while 33 cases (94.3%) showed ear fullness, which was the most common symptom. Of the cases, 22 cases (62.9%) affected the right ear and 13 (37.1%) 
the left ear. All cases were unilateral. According to the Teed classification, 16 cases (45.7%) were classified as Grade 0 and 11 
(31.4%) as Grade I. Grade III was diagnosed in seven subjects (20.0%); one student pilot (2.9%) with eardrum rupture was classified as Grade IV. The mean duration of illness was 5.9 d (1–21 d, median: 4 d). Symptomatic treatment was performed in 19 cases (54.3%) and the remaining 16 subjects (45.7%) were merely observed during follow-up (**Table I**).

Nine-step inflation-deflation tests were performed at the first visit of 34 subjects (97.1%): tympanometry could not be performed in the one case having Teed Grade IV. The mean value of resting MEP was 268.4 6 45.8 mmH2O. There were 29 cases (85.3%) who failed to pass the entire test. Among them, 19 cases (55.9%) failed in the postinflation test. Of note, four cases showed negative results in both eardrum findings and the nine-step test, despite persistent ear fullness postflight. Two of these subjects demonstrated an improvement in symptoms in 1 d and the other two exhibited symptom improvement on days two and three, respectively. A nine-step test was performed again after middle ear barotrauma clinically improved. All patients passed this test; initial TM rupture normalized in the subject with Teed Grade IV. On follow-up testing, the mean resting MEP was 215.9 6 19.1 mmH2O. Eight patients (22.9%) 
failed to pass the total nine-step test, while six (17.1%) of the eight subjects could not pass the postinflation test (**Table II**).

## Discussion

The ET, which regulates MEP, acts as a one-way valve with the proximal cartilaginous portion of the tube. The ET collapses and opens involuntarily when middle ear pressure exceeds surrounding pressure.11,12 ET dysfunction is the most common etiology of middle ear barotrauma.9 Middle ear barotrauma may occur under conditions that inhibit the function of the ET 
or when the velocity of pressure change surpasses tube competency.4 Failure to equilibrate MEP may increase the pressure differences such that the middle ear mucosa swells, and hemorrhagic transudates fill the middle ear cavity.12 Therefore, assessing the subclinical dysfunction of the ET 
was important to reduce the related risk of middle ear barotrauma. Assessment was especially important for recently recovered pilots to minimize grounding. Several noninvasive methods have been used to estimate ET function.3,14,20 One of the simplest ways to appraise ET function is to assess certain symptoms and visually inspect the TM. Middle ear effusion or retraction of the TM in patients with ear fullness, otalgia, or conductive hearing loss is presumptive evidence of ET dysfunction. However, identification of the degree of dysfunction is only possible with the appearance of the ear drum, but normal

Valsalva and Toynbee tests are classic tests of tubal patency and are easy to perform, but these tests have been reported to show inaccuracy in predicting middle ear barotrauma.20 Both tests were performed by comparing a baseline and experimental tympanogram obtained before and after Valsalva and Toynbee maneuvers.17 Recently, several studies have introduced distinct ET tests such as manometry, sonotubometry, and tubomanometry. Manometry has commercial limitations but is still clinically acceptable for assessing ET function in cases without intact TMs. Sonotubometry shows a high degree of correlation with tympanometry in evaluating ET 
function.8 Nevertheless, several authors have reported that sonotubometry is difficult and can have poor reproducibility.5,13 Some recent studies demonstrated that tubomanometry performed even better than tympanometry.7 However, there is no consensus on its application and use in practice.2 The nine-step inflation-deflation test is simple and usually provides useful information about ET function. Uzun et al.20 investigated the efficiency of the nine-step test and demonstrated the highest efficacy (sensitivity: 71%, specificity: 92%) 
and predictive values (positive predictive value: 63%, negative predictive value: 94%) for predicting middle ear barotrauma. This was observed in scuba divers in a study that compared the nine-step test to Valsalva and Toynbee tests. The nine-step test also has outstanding sensitivity (89%) and specificity (100%) 
values in predicting middle ear barotrauma for aircrew members.6 However, Karahatay et al.10 reported that the nine-step inflation-deflation test was not predictive of middle ear barotrauma after hyperbaric oxygen therapy. This result was presumably due to the limitation that the experimental nine-step test was conducted 7 d after hyperbaric therapy.

In the present study, a "poor result" was verified in 29 cases with the overall test and 19 cases with the postinflation test at first visit. Therefore, the sensitivity value was 85.3% in the overall test and 55.9% in the postinflation test. The specificity and negative predictive values of the initial test were not confirmed since this study was conducted on affected patients without normal controls. The sensitivity and specificity values predicting recurrence among pilots who recently recovered from middle ear barotrauma were 100.0% and 96.4% for the entire nine-step inflation-deflation test, respectively. 

Test sensitivity showed discordance between initial and followup tests, at 85.3% and 100%, respectively. This may be due to the four cases (Cases 5, 6, 9, and 21 in Table I) with a negative result in both eardrum findings and nine-step test. The sensitivity of the initial test, which was confirmed in the other 30 cases (with the exception of the 4 cases), was 96.7%. Positive and negative predictive values were 87.5% and 100.0%, respectively. The sensitivity and specificity values for the inflation test alone were 85.7% and 100.0%, respectively.

Positive and negative predictive values of follow-up tests were 100.0% and 96.6%, respectively. Follow-up nine-step testing demonstrated a high correlation with recurrence of middle ear barotrauma (Tables III and IV). The duration of middle ear barotrauma, postinflation test results on initial nine-step tests, and resting MEP on initial and follow-up tympanometry also showed a correlation with recurrence.

The present study involved a retrospective chart review of subjects with middle ear barotrauma and has the major limitation that a normal control population was not included. The small sample size of only 35 subjects is another possible limitation. In addition, the four cases with negative eardrum and nine-step tests could result in bias. It is reasonable to categorize barotraumas as a physiological phenomenon rather than a pathological disease in cases with temporary symptoms only during descent. However, those four cases complained of sustained ear fullness even after flight and therefore were included in the study. These cases might be categorized as a third state between a physiological event and true middle ear barotrauma. It would be essential to analyze such cases in the future.

The nine-step inflation-deflation tympanometric test appears to be a simple and effective method to diagnose middle ear barotrauma. The test is also effective for predicting recurrence of middle ear barotrauma. Even if symptoms disappear and a normal TM is visible in the affected pilot, ET dysfunction may not have completely resolved. The nine-step test may be more sensitive than visual inspection of the TM in evaluating ET function and may help to identify patients with a high risk of recurrence. Conducting nine-step inflation-deflation tympanometric tests may be preferable to other tests for barotrauma. In particular, the nine-step test proved to be especially effective in reducing the risk of barotrauma recurrence in pilots by preventing premature return to flight duty.
