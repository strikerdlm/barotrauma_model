# Acute Otitic Barotrauma During Hypobaric Chamber Training: Prevalence And Prevention

 Angelo Landolfi , Francesco Torchia , Alberto Autore , Mario Ciniglio Appiani , Fabio Morgagni , and Giuseppe Ciniglio Appiani L ANDOLFI A, T ORCHIA F, A UTORE A, C INIGLIO A PPIANI M, M ORGAGNI
F, C INIGLIO A PPIANI G. Acute otitic barotrauma during hypobaric chamber training: prevalence and prevention. **Aviat Space Environ** Med 2009; 80: 1059 - 62 . 

Introduction: Barotitis media is known to be an adverse effect of altitude changes, but few studies have investigated this condition with respect to hypobaric chamber training and the resulting estimations of prevalence vary. *Methods:* In order to assess the prevalence of hypobaric chamber-related barotitis and evaluate a method of prevention, 335 healthy military pilots undergoing high altitude training were subject to clinical examination and tympanometry before entering the chamber. In order to minimize the risk of barotrauma, only subjects with normal prefl ight fi ndings were cleared for altitude exposure. Barotitis media was diagnosed on the basis of ear pain and clinical fi ndings according to Teed's classifi cation. *Results:* Barotitis occurred in eight subjects; seven cases were monolateral and one bilateral, prevalence was 2.4%. *Conclusion:* The prevalence of barotitis after hypobaric chamber training is low in our study, suggesting that a pre-chamber medical check including clinical examination and tympanometry could be effective in identifying subjects at risk. Keywords: barotitis media , hypobaric chamber , ear barotrauma . 

Copyright: Aerospace Medical Association BAROTITIS MEDIA IS an infl ammation of the middle ear resulting from the effects of a difference between the pressure of the gases in the middle ear and that of the environment. It occurs in aviation and diving when the ambient pressure changes rapidly and pressure in the middle ear fails to equilibrate with external pressure ( 2 ). Although barotitis is a known adverse effect of fl ight and simulated exposure to high altitude, few studies have investigated the problem and reports of its prevalence vary widely. Ear pain is often considered a normal reaction and a further lack of information cannot be excluded as fl ight crews tend to minimize some disturbances to avoid repercussions on their certifi cation. 

 The pathophysiological mechanism is well-known 
( 10,7,13 ): the Eustachian tube must open to allow ventilation of the middle ear and eliminate the pressure difference. While the osseous part of the tube is normally patent, the cartilaginous part is closed because its walls collapse ( 4 ). Therefore, the proximal part of the tube acts as a one-way valve: during ascent it opens spontaneously as internal pressure exceeds ambient pressure, but during descent actions such as swallowing, yawning, or performing the Valsalva maneuver are required to open it ( 15,5 ). 

Barotrauma can occur when any condition interferes with the opening of the tube or the velocity of pressure change exceeds the capacity of the tube to ventilate the ear ( 9 ). 

 The eardrum, which normally bulges slightly inward, is overstressed as the pressure gradient increases, resulting in damage to both the eardrum itself and the middle ear mucosa. The anatomical changes include punctuate hemorrhages on the eardrum, serous exudates, and hemorrhage into the middle ear, while rupture of the eardrum can occur in more serious cases. These changes are responsible for common symptoms such as ear fullness, hearing loss, and ear pain. 

 Preventing barotrauma requires appropriate selection of subjects and rates of pressure change. The combined use of clinical examination and tympanometry before exposure appears to be a suitable method for assessing the functional capacity of the Eustachian tube in order to exclude subjects at increased risk of barotrauma. The purpose of this study was to investigate the prevalence of hypobaric chamber-related barotitis and evaluate the effectiveness of pre-chamber medical selection. 

## Methods

 We studied 335 subjects undergoing hypobaric chamber training in a period from March 2007 to October 2008 at the Aerospace Medicine Department of the Italian Air Force Flight Test Center. Data were collected from standard training operations and a set of noninvasive procedures was carried out before and after chamber exposure including: 1) clinical examination of ear, nose, and throat; 2) otoscopy; and 3) tympanometry. The study protocol was approved by the appropriate Institutional Review Board and each subject provided written informed consent regarding the additional procedures before participating.

All personnel admitted to the hypobaric chamber had to be certifi ed for fl ight duties or altitude exposure by the ITAF Aeromedical Institute. No physical activity, no diving, nor water survival training were permitted less than 24 h before the training. All subjects completed a questionnaire on their current physical conditions and were subject to clinical examination, including otoscopy and tympanometry by an otolaryngologist just before the altitude exposure. The tympanometry was used in particular to assess the effectiveness of the Valsalva maneuver. Subjects that did not pass the medical check were not cleared for the altitude exposure. 

 Before entering the chamber, all subjects also received a briefi ng by a doctor on the anatomy and physiology of the middle ear, and the validated methods for balancing pressure difference, for example the Valsalva, were fully explained and demonstrated. The hypobaric chamber profi les were widely used standards and included ascents up to 25,000, 35,000, or 43,000 ft ( 7620, 10,668, or 13,106 m), each followed by a sudden decompression session. For all profi les, a brief run up to 5000 ft (1524 m) followed by return to sea level was performed to check the ability of the trainees to equilibrate the internal pressure (sinus check). Subjects that did not pass the sinus check were discharged without completing the profi le. 

 To minimize the risk of barotrauma the maximum climb rate was set at 4000 ft z min 21 (1219 m z min 21 ) and the maximum descent rate at 2500 ft z min 21 (762 m z min 21 ). The chamber was operated to allow emergency arrest on demand if a subject reported ear or abdominal pain, or any other disturbance possibly related to the altitude changes. Barotitis media was diagnosed in subjects with ear pain during or immediately after the altitude exposure and otoscopic fi ndings consistent with Teed's classifi cation: Grade 0, no sign of barotrauma; Grade I, eardrum retraction with redness in Shrapnell's membrane and along the manubrium; Grade II, eardrum retraction with hyperemia of the entire eardrum; Grade III, hemotympanum and/or fl uid level in the middle ear; and Grade IV, eardrum rupture ( 17 ). 

 The tympanogram provides information regarding the compliance of the middle ear system and the functional capability of the Eustachian tube. It plots the compliance with the artifi cially altered pressure in the auditory canal expressed in millimeters of water 
(mmH 2 O), based on the principle that the maximum compliance occurs when the external and the internal pressures are equal. The commonly used classifi cation includes: Type A - tent shaped curve with a high peak between 1 50 and 2 100 mmH 2 O (normal pattern); Type B - abnormal fl at curve; Type C1 - negatively shifted curve with peak between 2 100 and 2 200 mmH 2 O; and Type C2 - negatively shifted curve with peak exceeding 2 200 mmH 2 O. Types C1 and C2 indicate negative pressure in the middle ear space, with C2 being associated with more severe conditions. Type B is consistent with fl uid or infection in the middle ear space and could be associated with a hole in the eardrum ( 8,18 ). 

## Results

 The population group included 329 (98.2%) men and 6 (1.8%) women, ages 20 to 54 yr (mean 32.4 yr). Four subjects who did not pass the sinus check and left the chamber before starting the ascent were not included in the analysis. Eight male subjects reported ear pain on descent (seven monolaterally and one bilaterally), resulting in a prevalence of 2.4%. Monolateral barotitis was found in 3 subjects (1.3%) out of 226 exposed to 25,000 ft (7620 m) and in 4 subjects (3.8%) out of 107 exposed to 35,000 ft (10,668 m). One subject (50.0%) out of two trainees exposed to 43,000 ft (13,106 m) had bilateral barotitis. All the episodes occurred during descent between 18,000 and 3000 ft (5486 and 914 m). On the basis of Teed's classifi cation, the subject affected by bilateral ear pain had a third-degree barotitis on the right side, with evidence of fl uid behind the eardrum and retraction with hyperemia of the entire eardrum on the left side, corresponding to second-degree barotitis ( **Table I** ). ## Discussion

 Barotitis media is widely reported during fl ight in pilots and passengers exposed to changes in barometric pressure. Barotrauma has also been indicated as a significant contributor to several mishaps involving USAF 
aircraft ( 11 ). Conversely, few studies have investigated the prevalence of barotitis media during hypobaric chamber training. The major fi nding of our study is the low prevalence of altitude chamber-related barotitis media among prescreened subjects. 

 Rosenkivst investigated the prevalence of ear barotrauma among commercial pilots and found that more than one-third of individuals experienced one or more episodes of barotrauma in their career ( 14 ). The prevalence of barotitis in passengers after fl ight has been reported to vary from 8 to 17% ( 16 ). The reported prevalence of acute barotitis during hypobaric chamber exposures is also different among several studies. Al-Wedyan and colleagues reported a 3.5% prevalence of ear block ( 1 ) and Ohrui and colleagues reported a prevalence of ear pain of 4.7% in a 39-yr study in Japan ( 12 ). Both studies indicate a higher prevalence compared with our data, but a 1-yr retrospective study from the U.S. Navy showed an incidence of 2.1% ( 6 ). The variable occurrence is explained by different diagnostic criteria, profi les, and altitude change rates adopted by several operators. 

 Any event that could interfere with the ventilation of the middle ear can facilitate barotrauma. Because personnel admitted to altitude training are generally healthy and certifi ed for fl ight duties, our opinion is that the most common cause of altitude chamber-related barotitis is dysfunction of the Eustachian tube not necessarily linked to a pathological state. Indeed, the causes include nondetectable infl ammation and incorrect or delayed performance of the Valsalva maneuver during altitude changes. 

 In adults, tube dysfunction is often associated with upper airways infl ammation or allergic rhinitis and the risk of barotitis is increased in patients suffering from these conditions, but not in allergic individuals who are asymptomatic. All our subjects can be clearly considered as normal because they showed normal clinical exami-

The barotitis in our subjects could be related to a very slight obstruction of the tube lumen due to focal infl ammation not detectable by either otoscopy or tympanometry. In effect, an abnormal tympanogram correlates highly with impaired tube function, but a normal tympanogram does not necessarily indicate normal function. 

 Despite the training received, an individual may have delayed performing the Valsalva or performed it incorrectly. The resulting failure to equilibrate the middle ear pressure may have facilitated the temporary ear block, for which further balancing maneuvers were ineffective. Some considerations could also be made regarding the safety of descent rate, which in any case we set to balance the effectiveness of the training and the risk of barotrauma. We chose altitude change rates that appear to be safe based on the experience of other operators. 

Tympanometry has proven useful for confi rming barotitis after fl ight, but no studies have demonstrated its possible role in identifying subjects at risk ( 3 ). The combined use of clinical examination and tympanometry could be more effective and our data seems to be consistent with this hypothesis. 

 The major limit of our study is that eight cases are not suffi cient for analysis with statistical tools. We did not take cost effectiveness into consideration because we have the favorable circumstances of a small number of trainees and several specialists on site. The described pre-chamber medical selection is probably expensive and somewhat unpractical for facilities where many people are to be trained on the same day, but it could be effective in minimizing the risk of barotrauma. We did not investigate whether a technician instead of an otolaryngologist could effectively carry out the medical selection in order to contain the resulting cost. This is likely for the tympanogram, which in any case must be interpreted by a specialist. Conversely, in otoscopy the operator's experience is directly benefi cial and it would, therefore, be better for a trained doctor to perform this procedure. 

 In conclusion, the prevalence of barotitis related to altitude chamber training is low among Italian Air Force fl ight crews. Pre-chamber medical selection, including the combined use of clinical fi ndings and tympanometry, could have an effective role in identifying subjects at risk. Because several predisposing conditions may be asymptomatic and not necessarily related to a pathological state, the evaluation should focus mainly on Eustachian tube function.
