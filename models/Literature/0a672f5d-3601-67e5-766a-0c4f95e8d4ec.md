## Introduction

Pressure differences between the middle ear and the external auditory canal (EAC) are normally equalized by the Eustachian tube (ET), which connects the middle ear to the back of the throat on each respective side. These tubes are usually closed, but are opened during airplane takeoff or landing to allow equalization of middle ear pressure [1–4].

Blockage of the Eustachian tube is called Eustachian tube dysfunction (ETD) [1, 4, 5].

Clinical manifestations of ETD are ear fullness, ear pain, and/or hearing loss or a clinically 'latent' condition, as recently reported by some studies about Eustachian tube function [6–10].

Flight, speaking, swallowing, or a self-inflation of the ears (Valsalva maneuver) are generally not effective in equalizing middle ear pressure in pilots with a Eustachian tube dysfunction (ETD) or if the velocity of pressure change exceeds the capacity of tube opening.

Therefore, the presence of a clinical evident or a 'latent' ETD, or tubal delayed opening, represents a serious risk for pilots of incurring in sudden onset of ear pain, fullness, or barotitis media during ascent or landing [4, 11–14].

Besides, pilots are subjected to wide swings in barometric pressure, causing continuous middle ear and ET barotraumatic stress. For this reason, barotraumatic injuries are possible during flight even in pilots who do not present ear symptoms or signs of tubal dysfunction at the common tests for assessing tubal function [14–16].

Although many studies have examined barotrauma related to air travel in civilians, only a few studies have analyzed this condition in airplane pilots: no specific study about ET function in air pilots has been performed [14–18].

Recently, tubomanometry (TMM) has been introduced into clinical practice as a specific test for the evaluation of ETD. It is a relatively simple instrumental examination that allows the assessment of ET and provides reliable information about the dynamic capacity of the ET patency under redefined conditions [7, 8, 19–22].

Ockermann et al. [7, 8] used TMM to develop a summation point score for ET function evaluation called Eustachian Tube Score (ETS). The ETS comprises three TMM
measurements and two subjective patient assessments concerning feasibility of Valsalva's and Toynbee's clinical symptoms. Several studies used this score to analyze the function of the ET, including Magliulo et al. [6], who employed the ETS formulated by Ockermann et al. [7, 8]
and diagnosed a clinically 'latent' ETD in a group of patients with primary antibody deficiency.

Recently, in a retrospective clinical study, Schro¨der et al. [9] extended the classic ETS scale formulated by Ockermann et al. [7, 8], including the results of tympanometry and objective Valsalva. Thus, the new ETS-7 utilizing further parameters gives a more precise evaluation of the Eustachian tube function.

The aim of this study was to assess ET functions in a group of military aviators using the new ETS-7. The limiting factor for correct ETD evaluation in aviators is that none of the ET test functions can be performed during the dynamic changes in atmospheric pressure which occur during flight. To overcome this limitation and to simulate the pressure variations of flight, we analyzed the ET
function in aviation pilots before and immediately after hypobaric chamber exposure. We used this procedure to: (1) identify any tubal dysfunction which might cause

## Materials And Methods

Forty-two pilots, members of the Italian Air Force, were enrolled in our clinical prospective study. Thirty-nine were male and three were female, aged between 21 and 44 (average age 31.1 years). All aviators underwent hypobaric chamber training to simulate pressure variations during flight, especially during takeoff and landing.

None of the Air Force pilots studied had been diagnosed with chronic ETD, because an established diagnosis of ETD would have implied an immediate flight disqualification with the request of a waiver from flight duties.

A questionnaire to evaluate the medical history of each studied pilot was administered immediately before the instrumental examination and hypobaric pressure chamber exposure, to collect data about any previous middle ear disease. Middle ear pathologies, in particular previous barotitis, ear fullness episodes during flight, otitis media with effusion (OME), and suppurative otitis media (SOM), were investigated.

Each subject was submitted to an ENT clinical examination before and after chamber exposure.

The Teed classification of middle ear barotrauma was adopted to evaluate the status of the tympanic membrane before and after hypobaric chamber exposure [23, 24]
(Table 1).

The Eustachian tube score (ETS-7), recently introduced by Schroder et al. [9], was adopted to evaluate the functionality of the Eustachian tube before and after exposure to hypobaric chamber.

A pathological tympanogram or a pathological tympanic membrane in accordance with the Teed classification represented criteria for exclusion from hypobaric chamber due to the risk of barotitis [23, 24].

For comparison of clinical and tests results between the different groups, the Student's T test and the Mann–
Whitney test were used for parametric or non-parametric analyses by SAS, JMP8 version (SAS Institute, Cary, NC,
USA). A p value of\0.05 was taken as the threshold value for statistical significance.

This research was performed in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and approved by our Aerospace Medicine Department review board.

## Hypobaric Pressure Chamber

All aviators underwent the hypobaric chamber training session at the Aerospace Medicine Division.

Pilots were located inside the hypobaric chamber at ground level (i.e., at sea level; barometric pressure:
760 mmHg). All 42 subjects were trained with a session consisting of an ascent up to 25,000 ft at 4000 ft per min followed by a demonstration of acute hypoxia, then a descent to 18,000 ft for demonstration of visual impairment in darkness due to hypoxia, and finally, a return to ground level at 2500 ft per min (Fig. 1).

The simulated altitude selected for this research reduces the environmental oxygen partial pressure to 59.2 mmHg, in contrast to the 159.2 mmHg value at ground level, perfectly simulating the phases of takeoff and landing of a flight with any possible associated middle ear symptoms [25].

All trainees were asked to periodically swallow during the ascent or perform a Valsalva maneuver on descent, as is usual in the altitude training protocol.

## Eustachian Tube Score (Ets-7)

The ETS-7 as designed by Schroder et al. [9] comprises

five parameters able to assess the function of the ET: (1) tubomanometric measurements (TMM) of the ET function at three different pressure levels (30, 40, and 50 mbar); (2)
subjective estimations concerning the feasibility of Valsalva's symptoms; (3) subjective estimations of Toynbee's clinical symptoms; (4) tympanometry; and (5) objective Valsalva evaluation.

## Tmm

The concept of TMM is the controlled delivery of defined pressures of 30, 40, and 50 mbar to the nasopharynx through a nasal applicator [25, 26]. The pressure receptor probe is positioned in the ear canal, and the nasal applicator is positioned in both nostrils.

A pressure receptor probe located in the external ear canal registers pressure changes transmitted through movements of the tympanic membrane/perforated tympanic membrane. If the ET opens during swallowing, the defined pressure applied to the nasopharynx is transmitted into the middle ear spaces. Pressure curves of the nasopharynx and the ear canal are displayed on the monitor of the TMM device, and various measuring values are calculated [25, 26].

Diagrams are created that display the pressure changes in the nasopharynx and ear canal in millibars against the time in seconds. The opening latency index (R value) reflects the latency between pressure application in the nasopharynx and the measurement of a pressure change in the ear canal. This latency quantifies Eustachian tube function: immediate opening (R\1) indicating good Eustachian tube function and late opening (R[1) indicating restricted Eustachian tube function. No opening (R negative or not measurable)
indicates complete obstruction of the Eustachian tube
[7–9, 25, 26] (Fig. 2a–c).

## Ets-7 Score Calculation

The parameters adopted for the calculation of the ETS-7 as designed by Schroder et al. [9] are shown in Table 2. ETS7 ranges from a minimum of 0 to a maximum of 14 points.

Toynbee maneuver is made pinching nostrils during swallowing. It allows to equalize middle ear pressure giving the perception of a crackling sound during swallowing.

Positive Toynbee's clicking sound and positive Valsalva's maneuver are rated with 0 points when the answer is ''never'', 1 point for ''sometimes'', and 2 points for ''always''. The patients' specifications are supposed to relate to a period of at least the previous 2 months.

Regarding TMM results at 30, 40, and 50 mbar, an immediate opening of the ET (R\1) is given 2 points, a delayed opening (R[1) 1 point, and no opening (negative or not measurable R) 0 points.

The results of tympanometry are divided according to Jerger's classification [9, 25–27]: a type A curve is given 2 points, type C 1 point, and type B 0 points.

## Otoscopic Evaluation

In all pilots, the aspect of the tympanic membrane was compatible with a grade 0 of the Teed classification prior to undergoing the hypobaric chamber, because a higher score would have represented a criterion for exclusion from hypobaric exposure due to the risk of barotrauma.

After the hypobaric chamber, 38 (90.4 %) pilots had a normal tympanic membrane, while 3 (7.1 %) pilots and 1
(2.3 %) pilots showed grade I and grade III, respectively.

Prior to undergoing hypobaric chamber, all pilots were assessed by bilateral tympanogram. In all cases (100 %), a type A tympanogram was detected in both the ears.

The ETS-7 values were calculated for each ear in the entire group of 42 pilots.

In 39 (92.8 %) pilots, a bilateral normal value of ETS-7 of 8 or more was found.

## Ets-7 Post-Hypobaric Chamber

In all three aviators who presented a pathological ETS-7 before undergoing the hypobaric chamber session, these pathological data were confirmed with a worsening in the ETS-7 values reported. Besides, in relation to the pathological side, one pilot who presented pre-chamber unilateral ETD showed a post-chamber bilateral pathological ETS-7.

## Discussion

In flight, as the aircraft cabin pressure decreases during ascent, middle ear air pressure may exceed the EAC
pressure, causing the eardrum to bulge outward. The expanding air in the middle ear forces the ET to open and, by letting the air escape, equalizes the pressure. During descent, the reverse happens, causing the eardrum to bulge inward. To remedy this often painful condition, which also causes a temporary reduction in hearing sensitivity, the pilot must periodically open the Eustachian tube to equalize pressure by the Valsalva manouvre. This procedure forces air through the Eustachian tube into the middle ear, thus reducing symptomatology [11–14, 28].

If, due to the presence of an ETD, pressure is not equalized, a middle ear barotrauma may develop. A mild manifestation of this condition is characterized by otalgia, sensation of fullness in the ear, and hearing loss. More severe cases can be accompanied by nausea, development of a haemotympanum or a tympanic membrane rupture [11, 29, 30].

In the light of to the above, Eustachian tube dysfunction diagnosis is more important in aviators. In airmen, ETD
represents a frequent cause of grounding, because the appearance of middle ear symptoms may make pilots unable to fly, reducing their own safety or that of passengers [11, 31–35].

In Air Force pilots, chronic ETD implies immediate flight disqualification and requires a waiver from flight duties. No treatment is currently approved as curative in terms of derogation. In cases requiring surgical procedures for ETD correction, the waiver possibility must be carefully assessed case-by-case, based on the type of ETD and the treatment performed. According to the US Air Force, a return to flight can be considered not earlier than 3 months after surgery if symptoms have entirely disappeared [36].

The granting of medical certification in civilian aviators depends on the individual airman's symptomatology, especially at the time of their medical examination. Each pilot is required to self-certify whether episodes of ear block occur during the flight. They can often get a waiver if proper treatment is performed [35].

What is, therefore, the true incidence of ETD in aviators? The incidence of ET dysfunction in adults ranges from about 1–5 %. However, owing to the risk of grounding when a diagnosis of ETD is made, many aviators tend to omit episodes of in-flight ear fullness indicative of a possible Eustachian tubal dysfunction, meaning that this percentage may be underestimated.

Besides, it is reasonable to assume that this percentage may differ in aviators who are continually submitted to pressure changes and severe ET stress [14–16, 29, 30]. A
revision of the clinical practice guideline for Eustachian tube dysfunction and otitis media contained in the aeromedical information system and showed 99 cases of ETD diagnosis with 69 % of them disqualified from flight [14]. However, despite the fact that many studies have examined barotrauma related to aviation, no specific study regarding ET dysfunction in air pilots exists in the scientific literature.

With regard to this, it should be remembered that it is very difficult to obtain a precise estimation of ETD in aviators [12]. The main limiting factor for ETD evaluation in air pilots is that none of the ET test functions can be performed during the dynamic changes of atmospheric pressure which occur during the flight. Therefore, to assert that an ETD is present, and to evaluate the probability of barotitis during flight, the dynamic function of ET patency should be tested with specific tests during simulated flights as in a pressure chamber [14–16, 29].

To assess the prevalence of acute barotraumatic otitis in pilots, Landolfi et al. [14] analyzed 335 healthy military jet pilots during hypobaric chamber training. Barotitis occurred in eight subjects with seven monolateral and one bilateral cases with an incidence estimated at 2.4 %. The authors claimed that barotrauma occurs when any condition interferes with the opening of the tube or the velocity of pressure change exceeds the capacity of the ET to ventilate the ear. However, all tested pilots had clearly been considered as having normal ET function just before altitude exposure, because they showed normal clinical examination and had a normal tympanogram. Moreover, none of them reported ear pain or ear fullness during the sinus check.

How can this be possible? Landolfi et al. [14] postulated that these subjects barotitis could be related to a very slight obstruction of the tube lumen due to focal inflammation not detectable by either otoscopy or tympanometry.

With regard to this, many authors agree that the current diagnostic tests for assessing ET function, as routinely performed by ENT specialists (Valsalva test, tympanometry), cannot reliably evaluate ET dysfunction. A normal tympanogram attests the normal transmission of energy through the middle ear space and in many subjects with tubal dysfunction tympanometry is normal [9, 14, 19, 22]. Besides, the predictive value of this examination for barotrauma diagnosis has not been proven in any clinical study [22].

To estimate whether an early and asymptomatic tubal dysfunction was present in airplane pilots and if this condition could subsequently be responsible for episodes of barotitis, we analyzed the ET function in 42 air pilots before and after exposure in a hypobaric chamber.

We utilized TMM and the new ETS-7 score, recently introduced by Schroder et al. [9], to assess the ET functionality of these pilots. In our cases, despite the fact that all pilots had a tympanogram of type A according to the Jerger classification
(100 %) in both ears prior to hypobaric exposure, the results of TMM and the ETS-7 calculation showed different data. In 39 (92.8 %) pilots, a normal ETS-7 of 8 or more was found bilaterally, while, in 3 (7.2 %) cases, there was unilateral ETS-7 B 7. These data were better than those reported by Schroder et al., who observed an ETS-7 of 8 or more in 83.8 % of 215 healthy subjects with only 1.6 % of the ears presenting an ETS-7 score of 4 or less. This statistical difference can be explained by considering that the sample study comprised of airmen who underwent a selection process and followed training sessions to learn how to overcome barotraumatic stress on the Eustachian tube, induced by atmospheric pressure changes during aviation.

The evaluation of ET function after the hypobaric chamber session provided very interesting data. Nineteen percent of pilots had an ETS-7 B 7 with three bilateral and five unilateral cases. Three of those cases, two unilateral and one bilateral, were in subjects who also had pathological ETS-7 before undergoing the hypobaric chamber session. An abnormal ETS-7 score was reported ex novo in five pilots after the hypobaric chamber session. This shows how the stress of the hypobaric chamber and, consequently, the stress of the pressure variations that occur in flight are able to induce an injurious effect with possible inflammation of the Eustachian tube mucosa even in subjects not suffering from the previous Eustachian tube injuries.

During the training session in the hypobaric chamber, three of the pilots with post-chamber ETS-7 B 7 reported clinical evidence of ETD: two pilots had persistent fullness, while the other one had a barotitis, with persistent fullness, hearing loss, and ear pain, with otoscopic evaluation consistent with grade III of Teed's classification. The remaining five airmen reported an ear fullness which disappeared after performing Valsalva maneuver. In all those cases, the tympanogram remains invariable.

In conclusion, it is interesting to note that ETS-7 scores are more accurate than tympanometry alone not only for supporting the identification of clinically evident tubal dysfunction, but also for diagnosing a condition of ''latent'' ETD with high risk of barotitis in flight during takeoff or landing.

## Conclusion

The combined evaluation of middle ear symptomatology and ETS-7 before and after hypobaric chamber exposure appears to be a reliable method for assessing the hypobaric stress and the functional capacity of the Eustachian tube in aviators, to exclude subjects at increased risk of ear pain, fullness or barotrauma during flight. Further studies on a larger series are currently under way to confirm this hypothesis.
