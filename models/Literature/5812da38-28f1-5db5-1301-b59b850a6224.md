## Introduction

Aircrew are exposed to changes in environmental pressure as part of their occupation, and a normally functioning Eustachian tube (ET) is important for them to achieve middle ear ventilation and pressure equalization [1].

The Republic of Singapore Air Force (RSAF) employs a risk stratifcation approach for aircrew pre-employment medical screening (PEMS). For otorhinolaryngological conditions with the potential to cause ET dysfunction (ETD), applicants who are clinically assessed to be at low risk of otic barotrauma are accepted, while those assessed to be at high risk are rejected. In keeping with the occupational medicine practice of matching individual functional ability with the anticipated job demand [2], applicants assessed to be at intermediate risk are required to undergo a hypobaric chamber assessment—termed "trial of chamber" (TOC)—as a functional assessment. These applicants have either rhinitis symptoms but are not on regular medications, have previous ENT surgery, or are otoscopically observed to have abnormal tympanic membrane (TM) mobility on Valsalva manoeuvre. The applicants with abnormal TM mobility also undergo tympanometry.

We sought to identify factors associated with TOC failure among applicants with otorhinolaryngological conditions.

## Methods

All applicants to RSAF aircrew vocations who were assessed to be at intermediate risk of otic barotrauma over a 3-yr period were identifed using the RSAF Aeromedical Centre's electronic database. Their medical records, and the TOC assessment records of the subset of applicants who underwent TOC, were reviewed for demographic data, clinical fndings, and TOC outcomes.

Each TOC involved a hypobaric chamber profle exposing the applicant to an initial altitude peak of 10 000 ft (ascent of 4000 ft/ min, followed by a descent to 2000 ft at 2000 ft/min), and a second altitude peak of 18 000 ft (ascent and descent of 6000 ft/min). A doctor otoscopically examined the applicant after each descent for features of otic barotrauma based on Teed classifcation [3]. Applicants with signifcant symptoms or otoscopic fndings of any degree of otic barotrauma were deemed to have failed.

Data were analyzed using SPSS Statistics 23. The study was exempted from review by the DSO-Singapore Armed Forces Institutional Review Board (0015/2019).

## Key Learning Points What Is Already Known About This Subject:

- Aircrew are exposed to changes in environmental pressure as part of their occupational role, and a normally functioning Eustachian tube (ET) is important for them to achieve middle ear ventilation and pressure equalization.

- A risk stratifcation approach is typically employed for aircrew pre-employment medical screening (PEMS), and applicants with otorhinolaryngological conditions that have the potential to cause ET dysfunction (ETD) should be assessed for their otic barotrauma risk when exposed to environmental pressure changes.

## What This Study Adds:

- Hypobaric chamber-based functional assessment of aircrew applicants who are clinically assessed to be at intermediate risk of otic barotrauma is an effective PEMS approach.

- Individuals with lower weight and BMI are more likely to develop otic barotrauma when exposed to environmental pressure changes.

- Tympanometry cannot reliably replace functional assessment in a hypobaric chamber.

## What Impact This May Have On Practice Or Policy:

- For aircrew applicants who are assessed to be at risk of otic barotrauma due to possible underlying ETD, tympanometry should not be used in isolation to assess their ftness for aviation work.

## Discussion

We found that weight and BMI were independent predictors of TOC failure. Our study also showed that abnormal tympanometry was not a signifcant predictor of TOC failure.

A previous cross-sectional study of 82 otorhinolaryngology patients found that those with low BMI were more likely to have ETD [4]. Other studies of individuals with weight loss postbariatric surgery also found increased incidences of ETD [5, 6], with the likely pathophysiology being a decrease in the supportive Ostmann's fatty tissue surrounding the ET lumen. In this regard, we postulate that some of our applicants may have experienced recent weight loss at their time of TOC, which usually coincided with their physically intense basic military training.

The French Military Aviation Authority includes the use of tympanometry for all applicants [7] while we only performed tympanometry on applicants with abnormal TM mobility. Our fnding of a lack of association between abnormal tympanometry and failure of TOC is consistent with other studies [8–10]. 

There are also subtypes of ETD known to produce normal tympanograms [1] and it is plausible that some applicants with normal tympanograms may have had underlying ETD of these subtypes.

The absence of any longitudinal incidence of otic barotrauma among recruited applicants suggests that hypobaric chamber-based functional assessment of applicants at intermediate risk of otic barotrauma is an effective PEMS approach. 

While we acknowledge the short follow-up period, we believe that the fnding remains meaningful as any aircrew with a missed ETD of practical signifcance should symptomatically manifest during fying training.

This study has several limitations. The case series design renders it prone to selection bias, with only 25% of those who were initially assessed to require TOC eventually undergoing the functional assessment. Second, otoscopic examinations were subject to inter-observer variation. Finally, as with most PEMS encounters, the applicants may have under-reported their otorhinolaryngological history.

To our knowledge, our study is the frst to explore the relationship between individual-level factors and hypobaric chamber assessment outcomes among Asian aircrew applicants. Signifcantly, it found that individuals with lower weight and BMI are more likely to develop otic barotrauma. It also adds to the body of evidence that tympanometry cannot reliably replace functional assessment in a hypobaric chamber.
