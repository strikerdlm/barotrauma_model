We submit for consideration the **Original Article** entitled:

**"Middle Ear Barotrauma During Hypobaric Chamber Training: A Physics-informed, Externally Validated Risk Model"**

Middle-ear barotrauma (MEB) is the most frequent adverse event in military hypobaric-chamber training and a recognized proxy for the Eustachian-tube dysfunction spectrum that disqualifies aviator candidates and restricts operational employment. Despite a 20-year accumulation of validated single-event models and large cohort surveys, no validated quantitative prediction tool exists that an otologist or flight surgeon can apply at pre-chamber screening to estimate per-exposure risk for an individual patient or to recommend an individualized maximum safe descent rate. We present a physics-informed, pathophysiology-aware hazard model that integrates continuous descent-side Eustachian-tube aperture collapse, a six-state upper-respiratory-illness temporal modifier, a four-state Patulous Eustachian-tube model, and the Kanick-Doyle 2005 gas-exchange core. The model was calibrated to 7,271 hypobaric-chamber training exposures from the Colombian Aerospace Force (FAC) DIMAE registry (173 events; per-exposure prevalence 2.38%; Wilson 95% CI 2.06–2.75%) and externally validated — without any refitting — against three independent published Italian Air Force cohorts. Calibration reproduced the FAC anchor within 0.1 percentage points; external validation fell inside the observed Wilson 95% confidence interval for two of three Italian Air Force cohorts (Morgagni 2012, Landolfi 2009). To our knowledge this is the first TRIPOD-compliant quantitative MEB prediction model with multinational external validation, and the clinical outputs — individualized per-exposure risk and recommended safe descent rates — map directly onto otology decisions at the point of care.

The manuscript follows TRIPOD 2015 reporting guidelines for prediction-model development and external validation (28-item checklist provided as Supplemental Digital Content 1). Body text is approximately 3,250 words, with a 249-word structured abstract, 24 references, 2 tables, 4 figures, and 3 Supplemental Digital Content items — all within Otology & Neurotology Original Article specifications.
