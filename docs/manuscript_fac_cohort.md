# Sixteen-year incidence of ear barotrauma in a hypobaric-chamber training program at 2,640 m baseline altitude: Colombian Aerospace Force 2010–2026 cohort

**Running head:** FAC CHAMBER BAROTRAUMA INCIDENCE

**Article type:** Research Article

**Word count:** body ≈ 3,400 · abstract 248 · references 12 · tables 4 · figures 2 (placeholders)

---

## Abstract

**Background.** Middle-ear barotrauma is the dominant medical complication of hypobaric-chamber training and a surrogate end-point for Eustachian-tube disease. Colombian Aerospace Force (FAC) training at the DIMAE chamber is conducted from a 2,640 m (8,530 ft) resting altitude — a regime under-represented in the international chamber-barotrauma literature.

**Methods.** We pooled two FAC operational datasets: the DIMAE 2010–2020 structured case registry (257 ENT events in 6,565 chamber exposures) and the 2025–2026 Microsoft Forms preflight evaluation + medical-director log (1,046 preflight submissions and 98 chamber runs covering 706 student-exposures across Feb 2025 – Apr 2026). Case classification (clinical barotrauma vs. sub-clinical blocking vs. O₂-induced aerotitis), fitness-decision normalization, and vital-signs plausibility filtering are documented. Wilson 95% CIs are reported for every proportion.

**Results.** Pooled clinical barotrauma incidence across the 16-year window is 173/7,271 = 2.38% per-exposure (Wilson 95% CI 2.06–2.75%), projecting to 6.97% over three career exposures. The 2010–2020 (2.45%, 2.11–2.86%) and 2025–2026 (1.70%, 0.97–2.95%) subsets have overlapping CIs; incidence is statistically unchanged. Preflight denial rate is 2.29% (23/1,004), concentrated in respiratory (29.0%) and ear/URI (15.2%) flags. URI-composite preflight prevalence is 5.93%. Fifty-six percent of preflight blood-pressure readings meet ACC/AHA 2017 stage-1 hypertension criteria; mean SpO₂ is 94.8%.

**Discussion.** Pooled incidence is comparable to published Italian Air Force cohorts (1.5–2.4%) despite the elevated baseline altitude, suggesting the residual chamber-stress dose at 25,000 ft dominates baseline-altitude effects. The pooled 2.38% per-exposure anchor supports an operational calibration prior for physics-informed middle-ear-barotrauma simulators.

**Keywords:** middle-ear barotrauma, hypobaric chamber training, aerospace medicine, Eustachian-tube dysfunction, high-altitude baseline

## 1. Introduction

Middle-ear barotrauma — the inability of the Eustachian tube (ET) to equalize middle-ear (ME) pressure against changing ambient pressure — is the most frequently reported medical complication of hypobaric-chamber training and simulated altitude exposures.¹⁻³ In operational military aviation, chamber barotrauma serves three functions: (i) as a direct medical outcome to be minimized, (ii) as a surrogate screening end-point for Eustachian-tube disease that disqualifies aviator applicants, and (iii) as the primary calibration anchor for physics-informed middle-ear models.⁴⁻⁶

International incidence estimates span a wide range. Italian Air Force cohorts report 1.5% (Morgagni 2010, n = 1,241)¹ to 2.3–2.4% per-exposure (Morgagni 2012, n = 314; Landolfi 2009, n = 335).²·³ North American surveys of commercial pilots report 30–85% lifetime prevalence, dominated by in-flight pressurization changes rather than chamber training.⁴ Every one of these cohorts is trained at or near sea-level baseline altitude. No published series documents middle-ear barotrauma incidence in a hypobaric-chamber program conducted from a high-altitude baseline, despite Latin American, Andean, and Himalayan military aviation medicine programs routinely operating in this regime.

The Colombian Aerospace Force (Fuerza Aérea Colombiana, FAC) conducts chamber training at the DIMAE facility in Bogotá (2,640 m, 8,530 ft), the highest institutional baseline of any major national aerospace-medicine program. This is a unique epidemiological situation: aircrew arrive at the chamber already at an altitude that elsewhere would be the chamber's first hold point, and the descent phase terminates at 2,640 m rather than sea level. Whether this halves the descent-side pressure excursion (because the terminal altitude is higher) or raises the URI-driven ET-dysfunction baseline (because resident aircrew carry sub-clinical rhinosinusitis at a higher rate at altitude) has not been previously quantified.

We report a 16-year pooled incidence analysis of the DIMAE chamber cohort, combining the 2010–2020 structured case registry with 2025–2026 Microsoft Forms preflight evaluations and medical-director operational logs. The primary objective is to characterize per-exposure incidence and projected career-exposure risk across the pooled window. Secondary objectives are (a) to quantify preflight-denial discriminatory power across the DIMAE screening flags, (b) to describe the vital-signs and SpO₂ distribution of a high-altitude-resident aircrew population, and (c) to provide an operational calibration prior for physics-informed MEB simulators that must otherwise rely on internal or sea-level cohort estimates.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Setting and population

Chamber training at the Direccion de Medicina Aeroespacial (DIMAE) is conducted at Bogotá (2,640 m; P_ambient ≈ 560 mmHg). The chamber profile used during the study window is the FAC Bogotá default: 10-minute ascent at ~1,650 ft·min⁻¹ to 25,000 ft pressure altitude, 15-minute hold for hypoxia-recognition drills, and controlled descent at ~2,470 ft·min⁻¹ back to 2,640 m resting pressure. Training assigns crews to an INITIAL course (INICIAL; first chamber exposure) or a RECURRENT refresher (RECURRENTE; 3-year interval per FAC medical standard). Exposed populations include Colombian military aircrew (pilots, copilots, aeromedical evacuation crew, transport-aircraft crew, flight surgeons in training) plus a small fraction of non-FAC participants (contractors, liaison officers from other Latin-American services).

### 2.2 Data sources

**Dataset A — 2010–2020 structured case registry.** A curated 257-case Excel database (`Barotrauma.xlsx`) maintained by the DIMAE medical team over a 10-year window, with columns covering case identifier, flight date, TEED grade where available, lesion category (BAROTRAUMA, AEROTITIS POR OXIGENO, BLOQUEO, miscellaneous), demographics, entity (FAC / foreign / civilian), chamber profile type (SPN / IV-A / RD), anatomic segment of symptom onset (DESCENSO / TIERRA / TARDIO / HIPOXIA / ASCENSO), altitude at onset, medical-history antecedents, action taken during and after the flight, treatment rendered, disability outcome, and sequelae. An accompanying 2022 internal draft manuscript (Correa Guarín & Jaimes, unpublished DIMAE) attested an exposure denominator of 6,565 over the 2010–2020 window; this is the authoritative per-flight-exposure count used in the present analysis.

**Dataset B — 2025–2026 operational capture.** Two Microsoft Forms instruments were deployed in production Feb 2025: (i) a preflight medical-evaluation form (1,046 submissions Feb 3 2025 – Apr 14 2026) capturing role, rank tier, training type, specialty, aircraft assignment, age, 14 yes/no medical-screening flags with optional free-text specification, vital signs (PAS systolic, PAD diastolic, FC heart rate, SpO₂ oxygen saturation), physical-examination findings, fitness decision (Apto / No apto para entrenamiento), and an optional in-flight medical-event free-text field; and (ii) a medical-director chamber-run log (98 runs Feb 17 2025 – Apr 13 2026) capturing profile type, initial and final O₂-system pressures, student attendance count, inter-service notification checklist (Hospital Militar Hyperbaric Chamber, CATAM Sanidad), nine-stage event checklist, three emergency-category flags (medical / technical / safety), and Spanish free-text narratives describing medical events.

Raw files contain Colombian national identification (Cédula), first and last names, institutional emails, and mobile phone numbers. All analyses were performed on de-identified working copies; aggregate outputs under `docs/Cohort FAC/analysis/` have Cédula, names, emails, and phones stripped, ages bucketed to 5-year bands, and top-10 units retained.

### 2.3 Case definitions

Clinical barotrauma (primary numerator) is defined as a middle-ear pressure-equalization failure during chamber descent or within 72 h post-descent, documented by a DIMAE flight surgeon via otoscopy (Teed grading where performed) or by characteristic symptoms (otalgia, aural fullness, transient hearing loss). Oxygen-induced aerotitis (AEROTITIS POR OXIGENO; 22 cases in 2010–2020) is treated as a separate pathology and excluded from the clinical-barotrauma numerator. Sub-clinical ear blocking (BLOQUEO; 45 cases in 2010–2020) without a flight-surgeon-documented lesion is excluded from the strict numerator but retained in a secondary all-comers analysis. Foreign-military and civilian cases (27 in 2010–2020) are excluded from the FAC-only numerator; pooled estimates retain this exclusion for consistency.

### 2.4 Denominator construction

**2010–2020:** the 6,565 chamber-exposure count attested in the Correa/Jaimes 2022 DIMAE draft Methods is used directly as a per-flight denominator. Whether this count is person-flights (exposures) or unique individuals is not explicitly stated in the source document and is treated as the former per author convention. We acknowledge this is a single-sourced attestation and flag the limitation in §5.

**2025–2026:** the per-flight-exposure denominator is the director-reported NUMERO DE ALUMNOS summed across the 98 chamber runs (706 student-exposures). The preflight form captured 1,046 submissions against 981 cleared-to-fly decisions, reflecting multi-submission-per-student artifacts and pre-flight cancellations; we do not use the preflight denominator for incidence because it over-counts.

### 2.5 Statistical analysis

Wilson 95% confidence intervals⁷ were computed for every proportion: per-exposure barotrauma incidence, preflight denial rate, screening-flag prevalence, medical-emergency-per-run rate, and blood-pressure category prevalences. The Wilson interval was preferred over the normal-approximation Wald interval because several proportions fall in the low-prevalence regime (<5%) where the Wald interval is known to undercover. Career-exposure projections were computed as 1 − (1 − p)ⁿ with n = 3 (the number of chamber exposures scheduled across a standard FAC pilot career: initial + two recurrents), under the independence assumption discussed in §5. Pooled 2010–2026 estimates were computed by summing numerators and denominators across the two windows and reporting a single Wilson interval over the pooled proportion; no Bayesian shrinkage or meta-analytic combination was performed.

Narrative classification of the 19 director-logged medical events used a regex scan over a curated list of Spanish ENT terms (otalgia, barotrauma, plenitud, timpánica, aerocolia, hipoxia, paradójica, ansiedad) with manual review of ambiguous assignments. Fitness-decision normalization collapsed the 4 observed strings ("SI" / "APTO" / "Apto" / "SI\\nSI\\nSI\\nSI\\nSI") to "apt" and the 23 denial variants to "denied"; remaining edge cases ("PENDIENTE" / "R" / "S" / missing) were excluded from the denial-rate denominator. Vital-sign plausibility filters (PAS 70–220 mmHg; PAD 40–140 mmHg; FC 30–180 bpm; SpO₂ 70–100%) removed 8 of 1,046 rows with data-entry errors (e.g., PAS = 1,240 mmHg). Age text entries ("MIGUEL ANGEL" in the EDAD column, 9 rows) were coerced to NA via numeric parsing.

### 2.6 Ethics and data availability

The analysis is covered by a DIMAE ethics-board memo authorizing secondary analysis of the de-identified chamber-training registry. De-identified tidy CSVs (`phase1_2010_2020_tidy.csv`, `phase2_preflight_tidy.csv`, `phase2_director_tidy.csv`) and summary tables are available in the companion repository at https://github.com/strikerdlm/barotrauma_model under `docs/Cohort FAC/analysis/`. Raw name-level files remain under the DIMAE data-protection boundary and are not publicly released.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Pooled 2010–2026 incidence

Pooled clinical barotrauma incidence across the 16-year window is **173 / 7,271 = 2.38% per-exposure** (Wilson 95% CI 2.06–2.75%; Table I), projecting to **6.97% over three career exposures**. The 2010–2020 subset alone yields 161 / 6,565 = 2.45% (Wilson 95% CI 2.11–2.86%; 7.18% career-3 projection); the 2025–2026 subset yields 12 / 706 = 1.70% (Wilson 95% CI 0.97–2.95%; 5.00% career-3 projection). The 2025–2026 Wilson interval fully encompasses the 2010–2020 point estimate; incidence is statistically unchanged across the 15-year span.

**Secondary numerator strategies** are reported in Table I for comparison and reader auditing. Including the 45 sub-clinical BLOQUEO events raises the 2010–2020 all-comers incidence to 3.91% (Wilson 95% CI 3.47–4.41%; 11.29% career-3). Including the 22 oxygen-induced aerotitis events yields a barotrauma-plus-aerotitis composite of 3.17% per-exposure. A student-only numerator (excluding 43 non-student cases) produces 2.50% on an inflated 6,565 denominator; the true student-only rate is higher, and the magnitude of the under-estimate scales with the instructor share of the 6,565 (see §5).

### 3.2 Demographics (2010–2026 pooled)

The pooled case series comprises 269 events from which 226 (84%) were FAC aircrew, 214 (79%) were students versus instructors (87%) in the 2010–2020 subset, 817 (78%) were students in the 2025–2026 subset against 229 internal observers (22%; no explicit instructor category). Sex distribution was predominantly male (90% of 2025–2026 cases, per the FAC aircrew intake). Median age was in the 30–34 band; the cohort age distribution concentrates in the 25–44 range typical of operational military aircrew. Top specialty categories are transport crew (TTV), pilot (PILOTO), aeromedical evacuation (APH), and training instructors (TIR). Top aircraft assignments are UH-60, C-208, HUEY II, King 350, ATR, and AC-47T (2025–2026 subset only; 2010–2020 aircraft assignment was not uniformly recorded).

### 3.3 Preflight evaluation — screening-flag prevalence and denial discrimination

Preflight screening flag prevalences (Table II) stratify high-discrimination from low-discrimination flags. The highest prevalence flags are chronic_disease_or_hosp (4.97%, 52/1,046), ear_URI_history (3.44%, 36/1,046), recent_respiratory (3.25%, 34/1,046), and cardiac_hypertension (2.96%, 31/1,046). Denial rate stratified by flag is reported in Table III. The denial rate overall is 23/1,004 = 2.29% (Wilson 95% CI 1.53–3.41%), with per-flag discriminatory power ranging from 100% (malaise_fever_fatigue, 3/3; anemia_bleeding, 1/1) through 29.0% (recent_respiratory, 9/31) and 15.2% (ear_URI_history, 5/33) down to 0% (claustrophobia, 0/5; GI_symptoms, 0/11; diving_altitude_48h, 0/9; seizures_epilepsy, 0/2). High-discrimination flags (respiratory, ear/URI, fever/malaise, dental, cardiac) concentrate the clinical denial decisions; low-discrimination flags are retained for population surveillance without operational gating utility.

URI composite preflight prevalence (pooling ear_URI_history ∪ recent_respiratory ∪ malaise_fever_fatigue) is 62/1,046 = 5.93% (Wilson 95% CI 4.65–7.53%). This is the operational baseline against which controlled-rhinovirus-challenge literature⁸⁻¹⁰ and URI-state modifier tables in physics-informed MEB simulators⁵ should be validated.

### 3.4 Vital signs at 2,640 m baseline

After plausibility filtering, vital-sign distributions at Bogotá baseline (Table IV, n = 1,004–1,007) are: PAS 126.7 ± 13.6 mmHg (median 125, IQR 117–135); PAD 79.9 ± 8.3 mmHg (median 80, IQR 74–85); FC 75.1 ± 12.6 bpm (median 74, IQR 66.5–84); SpO₂ 94.8 ± 2.3% (median 95, IQR 94–96). Blood-pressure categorization under ACC/AHA 2017 thresholds⁽¹¹⁾ shows 260 (25%) normal, 144 (14%) elevated, **379 (36%) stage-1 hypertension**, 207 (20%) stage-2 hypertension, 4 (0.4%) hypertensive crisis, and 8 (0.8%) hypotension. Over half (56%) of preflight readings meet stage-1-or-worse criteria. This finding, publishable on its own under a preflight-screening lens, cannot be interpreted as clinical hypertension prevalence in the cohort without paired office-vs-ambulatory validation; the single preflight reading plausibly reflects a combination of genuine HTN, acute sympathetic response to the chamber environment, and measurement artifact.

SpO₂ at 2,640 m distributes as follows: expected-for-Bogotá (93–96%) 711 (68%), high-normal (≥97%) 177 (17%; possible supplemental O₂ or measurement artifact on a contact pulse oximeter), borderline (90–92%) 111 (11%), and clinically hypoxic (<90%) 5 (0.5%). The 94.8% mean is consistent with Beall-era reference values for acclimatized Andean residents.⁽¹²⁾

Denied-versus-cleared comparison: vital-sign distributions are essentially superimposed; denial is driven by the 14 yes/no flags and free-text findings, not by vital-sign thresholds. Mean FC is modestly higher in the denied group (80.5 vs 74.9 bpm), consistent with symptomatic respiratory and cardiac subgroups being over-represented in the denied population.

### 3.5 Director-logged chamber runs — event rates

Over the 14-month 2025–2026 operational window, 98 chamber runs produced 706 student-exposures (mean 7.2 students per run, range 1–24). Medical-emergency "SI" flags were recorded on **20 of 98 runs (20.4%, Wilson 95% CI 13.6–29.4%)**; approximately 1 in 5 chamber runs triggers a medical-emergency log entry. Technical-emergency flags appeared on 3 runs (3.1%); safety-operational-event flags on 16 (16.3%).

Narrative classification of the 19 interpretable medical-event narratives (one "NO" response excluded) yielded 12 clinical barotrauma events, 2 hypoxia-recovery events that exceeded training-protocol bounds, 1 claustrophobia/anxiety event, and 5 other (headache, paradoxical-oxygen reaction, sinus-related). Barotrauma-event narratives concentrate at the descent phase (9/12 described onset at altitudes 8,500–18,000 ft during descent with Valsalva unsuccessful), cover a wide altitude-at-onset range (8,500–25,000 ft), and report standard FAC-protocol interventions (level-off with slow descent, supplemental O₂, antihistamine + pseudoephedrine post-flight). Explicit Teed grading was present in only 3 of 12 narratives (0, I, II); the remaining 9 described symptoms without explicit grading — the same limitation affecting the 2010–2020 cohort (16 of 186 clinical barotrauma cases carry an explicit Teed grade, 8.6%).

### 3.6 Temporal trend 2010–2020 vs. 2025–2026

The Wilson 95% CI for the 2025–2026 incidence rate (0.97–2.95%) fully encompasses the 2010–2020 point estimate (2.45%); the 2010–2020 Wilson interval (2.11–2.86%) fully encompasses the 2025–2026 point estimate (1.70%). There is no statistical evidence of changing incidence across the 15-year span. The short 14-month 2025–2026 window and smaller 706 denominator widen the CI substantially but do not produce a trend finding. Pooling the two subsets yields the primary 2.38% per-exposure estimate reported in §3.1, which is the most precise 16-year summary and the recommended calibration prior for downstream modeling applications.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Principal findings

Clinical ear barotrauma incidence in the FAC DIMAE chamber is **2.38% per-exposure (Wilson 95% CI 2.06–2.75%) over the pooled 2010–2026 window**. Projected to three career exposures under an independence assumption, this corresponds to a career career-prevalence estimate of 6.97%. Incidence has remained statistically stable across the 15-year span between the 2010–2020 case registry and the 2025–2026 operational capture, despite institutional changes in data-capture methodology, a chamber-downtime period during the 2020–2022 COVID window, and resumption of training in 2025. Preflight denial rate is 2.29%, concentrated in high-discrimination respiratory, ear/URI, and fever-malaise flags, matching the expected pattern for a medical-screening tool. URI composite prevalence at preflight is 5.93%, providing a baseline against which controlled-rhinovirus-challenge literature and MEB-simulator URI-modifier tables can be validated.

### 4.2 Comparison with international cohorts

The pooled FAC 2010–2026 per-exposure incidence is comparable to published Italian Air Force cohorts: Morgagni 2010 reported 1.5% overall (n = 1,241, with 1.1% in the pre-screened subset and 2.7% in unscreened controls),¹ Morgagni 2012 reported 2.3% at 25,000 ft (n = 314),² and Landolfi 2009 reported 2.4% TEED-graded barotitis (n = 335).³ Our pooled 2.38% sits comfortably within this envelope despite the 2,640 m FAC baseline altitude versus the sea-level Italian AF baseline. Two mechanistic interpretations are compatible with this observation.

First, the chamber-descent-side pressure excursion — which is the principal driver of middle-ear barotrauma — depends on the descent rate and the pressure differential traversed, not on the absolute baseline altitude. A descent from 25,000 ft to 2,640 m traverses a smaller absolute pressure differential than a sea-level descent, but the fractional gradient-clearing task facing the Eustachian tube is similar. The residual chamber-stress dose appears to dominate baseline-altitude effects.

Second, a high-altitude-resident population may have a modestly different URI and sinonasal-mucosal baseline than a sea-level-resident population, but the effect on ET function has not been quantified in a controlled setting. The FAC 5.93% URI composite preflight prevalence is within the range reported for Colombian military populations in non-chamber contexts and does not suggest an elevated acute-URI burden specific to the Andean resident.

### 4.3 Blood-pressure finding and its scope limitations

The 56% stage-1-or-higher hypertension prevalence on the single preflight measurement is a salient finding that warrants follow-up but cannot be interpreted as a clinical HTN prevalence estimate. A single office-style preflight blood-pressure reading is known to over-estimate ambulatory blood pressure by 5–10 mmHg in active military populations under the white-coat effect, and the chamber pre-flight environment may amplify this via acute sympathetic activation. Before any clinical conclusion, a paired-measurement validation study (chamber-preflight reading vs. 24-hour ambulatory monitor, same subject) is required. We report the raw distribution as a cohort-surveillance finding and as a candidate screening lens, not as a clinical prevalence claim.

### 4.4 Limitations

**Denominator provenance.** The 2010–2020 denominator of 6,565 chamber exposures is single-sourced to the Correa/Jaimes 2022 internal DIMAE draft; the operational logbook that yields this count has not been independently cross-checked in the present analysis. If the 6,565 counts unique individuals rather than person-flights, the true per-exposure rate is lower and the career projection is closer to 2.45%; if it counts person-flights with an unpublished instructor-repeat fraction, the true student-only per-exposure rate is higher.

**FAC / non-FAC split.** The case-level data show 226/257 (88%) of 2010–2020 cases are FAC. If the 6,565 denominator has the same 88% FAC share, the FAC-only per-exposure rate is 161/5,777 = 2.79% rather than the 2.45% reported against the all-comers denominator. If FAC share of exposures is smaller (e.g., 70%), the true FAC-only rate is higher.

**TEED grading completeness.** Only 8.6% (16 of 186) of 2010–2020 clinical barotrauma cases carry an explicit Teed grade; only 3 of 12 (25%) 2025–2026 director-logged cases do. Per-grade stratification is therefore not possible in this registry, and we do not report a TEED-distribution finding. A structured Teed-grade field on future director forms would close this gap prospectively.

**Date completeness.** 45% of 2010–2020 case dates are year-only (`00/00/YYYY`); month or quarter-level trend analysis is valid only on the 115-case subset with full dates. Year-level trends are valid for all 257 2010–2020 cases, but per-year denominators are not available in the 2022 draft, so per-year incidence cannot be reported.

**Altitude at onset.** 28% of altitude-at-onset entries in the 2010–2020 registry are null or defaulted to the profile's nominal peak (10,000 ft for IV-A). We did not attempt to reconstruct altitude at onset from free-text narratives; the field's utility is limited until the registry is manually cleaned against the SEGMENTO (anatomic phase) column.

**Preflight / director reconciliation.** The 2025–2026 preflight form captured 3× fewer in-flight barotrauma events (4) than the director form (12) over the same 14-month window. Preflight captures are likely incomplete because students and flight surgeons may complete the preflight section before a delayed-onset event is recognized. We use the director form as the authoritative event-capture channel and do not combine events across forms.

**Independence assumption in the career projection.** The 1 − (1 − p)³ projection assumes three career exposures are independent, which they are not: the same trainee carries the same anatomy, ET function, chronic rhinosinusitis status, and career-stage URI environment across exposures. The true career rate under realistic correlation is lower than 6.97%. Absent a longitudinal per-trainee identifier that links exposures, this is a reportable limitation.

### 4.5 Clinical and modeling implications

For FAC aerospace medicine, the principal clinical implication is that the chamber training program at 2,640 m baseline operates at a per-exposure barotrauma incidence comparable to sea-level-baseline peer programs; the high-altitude-resident population does not appear to carry an elevated chamber-barotrauma risk. The high-discrimination preflight flags (respiratory, ear/URI, fever-malaise) are validated as appropriate operational gates; low-discrimination flags (musculoskeletal, dental, GI, substance) may be retained for surveillance without expecting denial-decision utility.

For physics-informed middle-ear-barotrauma modeling, the pooled 2.38% per-exposure / 6.97% career-3 provides a defensible calibration prior anchored in directly observed operational data. This supersedes the previously reported 5.8% career-only anchor used as an internal prior in FAC-calibrated MEB simulators,⁵ which underestimated per-exposure incidence by approximately 0.4 percentage points against the directly observed cohort. The updated anchor is used in the companion physics-informed simulator release (v2.2.1).

### 4.6 Conclusions

In the Colombian Aerospace Force DIMAE chamber training program at 2,640 m baseline altitude, pooled clinical ear-barotrauma incidence across the 2010–2026 window is 2.38% per-exposure (Wilson 95% CI 2.06–2.75%; 6.97% career-3 projection). Incidence is statistically stable across the 15-year window despite institutional data-capture changes and pandemic-period chamber downtime. Preflight screening flags concentrate denial decisions in high-discrimination respiratory, URI, and fever-malaise categories. The elevated baseline altitude does not appear to uplift chamber-barotrauma risk relative to sea-level-baseline peer programs; the descent-side chamber-stress dose dominates. These findings provide an operational calibration prior for physics-informed MEB simulators and a reference distribution for other high-altitude-resident aerospace-medicine programs.
